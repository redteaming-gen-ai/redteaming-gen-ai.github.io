ICML 2024 Workshop on

Red Teaming GenAI: What Can We Learn from Adversaries?






Overview

With the rapid development of Generative AI (GenAI), ensuring their safety, security, and trustworthiness is paramount. In response, researchers and practitioners have proposed *red teaming* to identify such risks, enabling their mitigation. Red teaming refers to adversarial tactics employed to identify flaws in GenAI-based systems, such as security vulnerabilities, harmful or discriminating outputs, privacy breaches, energy-latency attacks, and copyright law violations.


Recent research focuses on comprehensive evaluation frameworks for AI models, addressing safety and security risks. However, these efforts often provide only short-term solutions. The rapid evolution of AI necessitates ongoing updates to benchmarks to avoid them from becoming outdated due to models being excessively tailored to these benchmarks. Moreover, such evaluations must also incorporate the latest findings from AI safety research, which consistently expose new breaches in generative models. At the same time, other works point out the lack of consensus around the scope, structure, and assessment criteria for AI red teaming.


In response to the findings from red teaming exercises, researchers have taken action to curb undesirable behaviors in AI models through various methods. These include aligning the models with ethical standards, defending against jailbreak attempts, preventing the generation of untruthful content, erasing undesired concepts from the models, and even leveraging adversaries for beneficial purposes. Despite these efforts, a multitude of risks remain unresolved, underscoring the importance of continuous research in addressing the challenges identified through red teaming.


The goal of this workshop is to bring leading researchers on AI safety together to discuss pressing real-world challenges faced by ever-evolving generative models. We put a special emphasis on red teaming and quantitative evaluations towards probing the limitations of our models. Some of the fundamental questions that this workshop will address include (1) What are new security and safety risks in foundation models? (2) How do we discover and quantitatively evaluate harmful capabilities of these models? (3) How can we mitigate risks found through red teaming? (4) Can we make safety guarantees?

Jailbreaking defense competition

We will host a competition where participants compete to develop defenses against jailbreaking attacks. We will craft a collection of jailbreaking prompts against open-source models from existing literature, known to elicit bias harmful responses, or circumvent refusal mechanisms.


We will randomly select prompts from each category as a public set, and competitors or teams will then engineer defenses against these harmful prompts, for example using hidden prompts or fine-tuning. The proposed solutions will then be evaluated on held out prompts from each relevant category. A committee made of organizers will vote on the best three defenses with a scoring rubric that values effectiveness, practicality, and broad applicability. The top three teams will share a prize pool of 6,000$, generously sponsored by Arthur AI, and will also get a 10-minute lightning talk on their defense at the workshop. We will subsequently compile a paper containing submitted defenses, and participants will be included as authors. We will open the competition and release materials on May 15, 2024, and we will accept submissions until July 1.
